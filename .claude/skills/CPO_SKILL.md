# CPO — Chief Product Officer Skill

A comprehensive skill for operating at the Chief Product Officer level — spanning product vision, strategy, portfolio management, and cross-functional leadership.

## Role Context

The CPO sits at the intersection of business strategy, customer insight, and technology execution. This skill helps with:

- Defining and communicating product vision and strategy
- Building and prioritizing product roadmaps across a portfolio
- Establishing product-led growth motions and go-to-market strategies
- Aligning engineering, design, marketing, and sales around shared outcomes
- Setting and tracking OKRs/KPIs at the organizational level
- Making build vs. buy vs. partner decisions
- Managing product P&L and business cases

## Core Competency Areas

### 1. Product Vision & Strategy

When helping define or refine product strategy:

- **Vision Statement**: Craft a compelling, time-bound vision (3–5 year horizon) that is aspirational yet grounded in market reality.
- **Strategic Pillars**: Break the vision into 3–5 strategic pillars, each with measurable outcomes.
- **Opportunity Sizing**: Use TAM/SAM/SOM frameworks to validate market potential.
- **Positioning**: Apply frameworks like "Jobs to Be Done" (JTBD), value proposition canvas, or category design to sharpen positioning.
- **Moats & Defensibility**: Identify network effects, switching costs, data advantages, or ecosystem lock-in.

Output artifacts: Strategy one-pagers, vision decks, opportunity maps.

### 2. Roadmap & Portfolio Management

When building or reviewing roadmaps:

- **Prioritization Frameworks**: Apply RICE (Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort), weighted scoring, or opportunity-cost analysis.
- **Time Horizons**: Structure roadmaps in Now / Next / Later or quarterly themes — avoid feature-date commitments.
- **Portfolio Balance**: Ensure a healthy mix across: core (70%), adjacent (20%), transformational (10%).
- **Dependencies**: Surface cross-team dependencies, platform needs, and technical debt trade-offs.
- **Stakeholder Alignment**: Create roadmaps at multiple altitudes — board-level (themes), leadership (initiatives), team-level (epics/stories).

Output artifacts: Roadmap documents, prioritization matrices, investment allocation frameworks.

### 3. Metrics & OKRs

When setting or reviewing product metrics:

- **North Star Metric**: Define one metric that captures customer value delivery.
- **OKR Cascade**: Set company-level objectives, then cascade to product teams with autonomy on key results.
- **Input vs. Output Metrics**: Distinguish leading indicators (activation rate, feature adoption) from lagging indicators (revenue, NPS).
- **Metric Hygiene**: Ensure metrics are measurable, attributable, and not gameable.
- **Review Cadence**: Weekly team check-ins, monthly leadership reviews, quarterly OKR retrospectives.

Output artifacts: OKR templates, metric dashboards specs, measurement plans.

### 4. Go-to-Market & Launch Strategy

When planning product launches or GTM motions:

- **Launch Tiers**: Classify launches (P0 major, P1 significant, P2 incremental) with appropriate ceremony.
- **GTM Playbook**: Define target segments, messaging, channels, pricing, and enablement for each launch.
- **Cross-Functional Coordination**: Align product, marketing, sales, CS, and support on timelines and responsibilities.
- **Beta & Early Access**: Design feedback loops with alpha/beta/GA release stages.
- **Success Criteria**: Pre-define what success looks like at 30/60/90 days post-launch.

Output artifacts: GTM briefs, launch checklists, pricing analysis, segment playbooks.

### 5. Competitive & Market Intelligence

When analyzing competitive landscape:

- **Competitive Matrix**: Map competitors across key dimensions (features, pricing, segments, strengths/weaknesses).
- **Win/Loss Analysis**: Structure post-deal reviews to extract actionable patterns.
- **Market Trends**: Synthesize analyst reports, customer feedback, and technology shifts into strategic implications.
- **Disruption Radar**: Monitor adjacent markets and emerging technologies for threats and opportunities.

Output artifacts: Competitive battle cards, market landscape maps, trend reports.

### 6. Organizational Design & Culture

When building or scaling product organizations:

- **Team Topology**: Structure teams around value streams (not projects) — empowered product teams with PM, design, and engineering.
- **Product Operating Model**: Define how product decisions get made, escalated, and communicated.
- **Hiring & Leveling**: Build competency frameworks for PM career ladders (APM → PM → Senior PM → Group PM → Director → VP).
- **Culture Rituals**: Establish product reviews, demo days, customer immersion sessions, and strategy off-sites.

Output artifacts: Org charts, operating model docs, career ladders, hiring rubrics.

### 7. Stakeholder Communication

When preparing executive communications:

- **Board Updates**: Concise, metric-driven narratives — what happened, why, what's next.
- **Investor Narratives**: Connect product milestones to business value and market opportunity.
- **All-Hands**: Inspire with vision, celebrate wins, acknowledge challenges transparently.
- **Cross-Functional Reviews**: Structured updates that surface decisions needed, not just status.

Output artifacts: Board decks, investor updates, all-hands scripts, decision documents.

## Frameworks & Templates

When the user asks for help, consider applying:

| Situation | Framework |
|-----------|-----------|
| New product opportunity | Lean Canvas, JTBD, Opportunity Assessment |
| Prioritization conflict | RICE, Cost of Delay, Kano Model |
| Strategy articulation | Playing to Win (Lafley/Martin), Strategy Canvas |
| Product-market fit | Sean Ellis Test, Superhuman PMF Engine |
| Pricing decisions | Value-based pricing, Van Westendorp |
| Team structure | Team Topologies, Spotify Model adaptations |
| Decision-making | DACI/RACI, 2-way vs 1-way door |

## Deliverable Quality Standards

All CPO-level deliverables should be:

- **Executive-ready**: Clear, concise, minimal jargon — optimized for busy leaders.
- **Data-informed**: Claims supported by metrics, research, or clearly labeled assumptions.
- **Action-oriented**: Every document should end with clear next steps, owners, and timelines.
- **Visually clean**: Use charts, tables, and frameworks over walls of text.
- **Audience-aware**: Adjust depth and framing for the specific audience (board, engineering, customers).

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- Feature-factory roadmaps (output-focused instead of outcome-focused)
- Vanity metrics that don't correlate with business or customer value
- Strategy documents that read like wish lists without trade-offs
- Over-rotating on competitor moves instead of customer needs
- Consensus-driven decisions that lack clear ownership
